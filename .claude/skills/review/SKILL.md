# Blog Post Review

Review blog post drafts against three priorities, in order. Produce a structured review — not a rewrite. Flag specific problems with line-level references and suggest fixes inline.

## Priority 1: Readability (Scannability)

The reader should be able to half-read the post — eyes skimming, not deeply parsing — and walk away with basically the right point. This is the highest priority.

### What to check:

**Bold skeleton test.** Read only the bold text in the post. Does it tell a coherent story? If someone read *only* the bold phrases, would they get the main argument? Flag sections where bold is missing, overused, or marking the wrong words.

**Anchor paragraph test.** Look for short (5-8 word) standalone paragraphs that act as visual speed bumps. These should appear every few paragraphs. If there's a long stretch without one, flag it. If they're weak or generic ("Let me explain"), suggest a punchier replacement.

**Paragraph length.** Any paragraph over 4 sentences is a problem. Flag it. Most paragraphs should be 1-2 sentences. If a paragraph has 3-4 sentences, check that it earns the length.

**Section flow.** Can you tell what each section is about from the heading alone? Do sections follow a logical progression? Flag any section that buries its point.

**The squint test.** Imagine squinting at the post so you can only see the shape of the text — short blocks, bold words, headings. Does it look scannable or does it look like a wall? Flag walls.

### How to report readability issues:

For each issue, give:
- The location (section heading or paragraph number)
- What the problem is
- A specific suggestion (not "make it better" — show what "better" looks like)

## Priority 2: Interestingness (The Hook)

The title and intro must pull the reader in. The mechanism: **juxtapose modern-day intuition.** Say the thing the reader already half-believes but hasn't heard said out loud. Then complicate it.

### What to check:

**Title.** Is it provocative? Does it create tension or curiosity? A good title makes someone think "wait, what?" or "finally someone said it." A bad title describes the content. Flag descriptive titles.

**Opening line(s).** The first 1-3 paragraphs need to land hard. They should target the reader's unspoken thoughts — something they feel but haven't articulated. Check:
- Does the opener feel like it was written *for* the target reader?
- Does it create a "yes, but..." reaction?
- Is it bold enough? Or is it throat-clearing?

**The intuition gap.** Every post should push against some modern-day intuition. If you can't identify what intuition the post is challenging, that's a problem. **Ask Tim what he thinks the modern-day intuition is** so you can evaluate how hard the post pushes against it. This is not optional — ask.

**Intro minimalism.** The intro should orient the reader in 1-3 paragraphs and drop them in. If it takes more than 3 paragraphs before the post gets interesting, flag everything before that as cuttable.

**Analogies.** Are there analogies bridging from familiar territory to the new idea? Are they fresh or cliched? Do they earn their space?

**Thesis gravity.** Every section should pull back toward the post's main thesis. If a section wanders into supporting material (personal setup, technical detail, background), check whether it ties back. Short callout paragraphs at the end of sections are especially prone to floating — they should bridge the local point back to the central argument, not just label what happened. If a section could appear in a different post without changes, it's not tied tightly enough.

### How to report interestingness issues:

- For title: suggest 2-3 alternatives that are more provocative
- For openers: show what a punchier version could look like
- For the intuition gap: state what you think the post is pushing against and ask Tim if that's right

## Priority 3: Factuality (Armor, Not Accuracy)

This is NOT a fact-check. The goal is **not** to make the post more accurate. The goal is to make strong points *stick* — like an earworm. Memorable beats factual. But: anticipate where a reader will push back and preemptively address it, without hedging so much that the point dissolves.

### What to check:

**Vulnerable claims.** Identify claims that a knowledgeable reader would immediately object to. For each:
- State the likely objection
- Suggest a one-sentence steelman or preemptive acknowledgment
- Do NOT suggest removing the claim or softening it. Instead, suggest how to **hold the claim while acknowledging the counterpoint.**

**Over-hedging.** Flag any hedging language ("it might be", "perhaps", "arguably", "it's possible that") and ask: is this hedge load-bearing (acknowledging real uncertainty) or is it just throat-clearing? If throat-clearing, suggest cutting it.

**Under-hedging.** Flag any claim stated with absolute certainty that is easily disprovable. Suggest a way to hold the strong opinion while adding a crack of daylight ("This is almost always true — the exception being...").

**Stickiness.** For the 2-3 most important points in the post, evaluate: will this stick in someone's brain? Is it phrased memorably? If a point is true but forgettable, suggest a rephrasing that's more vivid, concrete, or surprising.

### How to report factuality issues:

For each issue:
- Quote the specific text
- State the likely reader objection or the stickiness problem
- Suggest a specific fix that keeps the opinion strong

## Review Format

Structure the review as:

```
## Readability

[Issues organized by severity]

## Interestingness

**What intuition is this post pushing against?**
[Your best guess — then ask Tim to confirm or correct]

[Title and opener feedback]
[Other interestingness issues]

## Factuality

[Vulnerable claims with suggested armor]
[Hedging issues]
[Stickiness suggestions for key points]

## Top 3

The three most impactful changes, in order:
1. ...
2. ...
3. ...
```

## What NOT to do

- Don't rewrite the post. Give targeted, specific feedback.
- Don't suggest adding summaries, conclusions, or "in this post we'll discuss..." framing.
- Don't flag style issues that match Tim's voice (fragments, starting with "But", casual abbreviations, internet-isms).
- Don't suggest making claims weaker. Suggest making them stronger *and* better armored.
- Don't suggest adding emojis, bullet-point summaries, or TL;DR sections.
- Don't praise what works unless it's genuinely surprising or non-obvious — Tim doesn't need validation, he needs useful critique.
- Don't apply generic "good writing" advice. Apply *Tim's* writing standards from the writing-style skill.
